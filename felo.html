Father Eugene Lafont Observatory (FELO)

Historic observatory advancing solar, lunar, and stellar studies.

“Fr Eugene Lafont observatory was built by a creative scientist and professor more than a century back at a time when science was nobody's business. It produced accurate results in solar and planetary observations and functioned as an exemplary instance of intercontinental collaborative science. Its incomplete journey is now again afloat after its revival in 2014 by a couple of unconventional professors and robust support of the college authorities and has been leading students, researchers, and the general public towards the appreciation of astrophysics as the grandest of science ever since. The observatory already has facilitates for stellar, solar and lunar observations and is open to everyone with an ear for receiving the cosmic symphony.”


–Dr. Shibaji Banerjee,
Assistant Director of FELO



The Father Eugene Lafont Observatory was established by Father Eugene Lafont, SJ, one of the founders of St. Xavier’s College, Kolkata, in 1866, and the creator of India's first scientific society. A priest, experimentalist, and pioneering educator, Fr. Lafont organized several public lectures, including one titled “The Magic Lantern,” where he projected an image using a pinhole camera onto a screen 12 feet away. He was deeply invested in scientific observation and, in November 1867, made careful barometric readings to track a cyclone, even warning the government about its destructive potential.


In 1875, a group of Italian astronomers led by Prof. Pierro Tacchini visited Bengal to observe the transit of Venus. Fr. Lafont assisted them by taking precise spectroscopic measurements and helping to accurately time the transit, a significant scientific accomplishment. Recognizing the need for long hours of sunlight to study the sun, the Italian team urged Fr. Lafont to establish an observatory at St. Xavier’s College, given its ideal location in the tropical zone.


“Father Lafont was a born scientific lecturer and had a peculiar facility for putting dry facts in a popular way and an equal facility for making his lectures interesting by excellent experimental illustrations”


-Obituary, Nature,14th May 1908.


The Father Eugene Lafont Observatory has recently embarked on its fascinating work. Under the able guidance of Dr. Shibaji Banerjee, the Assistant Director of FELO and the current Head of the Department of Physics, the observatory has flourished more, along with the enthusiasm of other Professors of the Department of Physics and the college administration alike.


The Astrometric Instruments present in FELO are :-

  1. Celestron CGE 1400 HD Telescope with accessories.
  2. Celestron 1400 HD Telescope with accessories.
  3. SBIG CCD Camera with accessories.
  4. Lhires-III Spectrograph with accessories
  5. Solar Measurement and Observation Kit.
  6. Artificial Star Temperature by Photometry.
  7. Solar Max III 70 mm with BF15 and Case.
  8. Meade 127/1000 Reflector Telescope.
  9. EQ Tracking Motor.
  10. Explore Scientific EX0S2-GT Equatorial Mount with PMC- 8 GOTO System.

TWIN OBSERVATORY

In addition to FELO, there is a twin observatory consisting of a stellar observatory with a motorised dome and a sliding roof observatory for solar observations. This facility is primarily used by MSc students specializing in Astrophysics for practical studies. During 2015-16, the observatory hosted several notable workshops, including collaborations with Jesuit institutions like St. Joseph's University in Bengaluru and specialized workshops on radio astronomy (RAWA), focusing on advanced topics in astronomy and astrophysics. In 2016, it closed for major refurbishments to address dome cooling issues for CCD and spectrograph observations.


The observatory reopened in 2017 and organized several night sky observation events during Lunar Week and National Science Day, attracting enthusiastic participation from students and faculty, including those from the B.Ed. department. It also welcomed participants from the Prayas Camp and the NAAC Peer Team. That year, the observatory acquired a CCD and a spectrograph through extended DST grants, facilitating test observations and research projects. Undergraduate student Soham Dey worked on Differential Stellar Photometry, measuring light from 8.5-magnitude stars, while postgraduate students Tanima Mondal and Sabyasachi Chakraborty focused on imaging an H-R diagram for the spiral galaxy NGC 230 in the Cetus constellation.


On October 28, 2017, the observatory organized the "International Observe the Moon" event for undergraduate and postgraduate students. Additional nightly observing programs took place on September 21st and 22nd, November 2nd, 2017, and January 9th and 10th, 2018, involving college and university teachers and students. The Total Lunar Eclipse was observed on January 31, 2018, followed by celebrations for National Science Day on February 28. These observing sessions concluded with a rural school outreach program organis by the Department of Social Work and NSS as part of the Prayas-2018 Camp.


Since the formation of the Xaverian Astronomical Society (XAS), the observatory has been utilized for multiple observation sessions for college and high school students as part of XAS’s outreach programs. Students from the Department of Physics and members of XAS have also conducted various research projects and papers using the facility.
