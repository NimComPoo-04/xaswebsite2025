Voyager 2025

“Astronomy is as much about the stars in the sky as it is about the fire of curiosity within ourselves.”

Voyager 2025 initiated the Xaverian Astronomical Society’s journey in the session 2025–26 and celebrated the remarkable contributions of Father Eugene Lafont in advancing astronomy at St. Xavier's College (Autonomous), Kolkata, through the customary Father Eugene Lafont Memorial Lecture. On September 16; the very first day of the Voyager 2025 program, the students and dignitaries gathered with a sense of anticipation and excitement at the Fr. Depelchin Auditorium. The day began with the solemn inauguration by Father Principal Rev. Dr. Dominic Savio, SJ, whose words set the tone for the intellectual journey that lay ahead and reminded everyone of the deep connection between human curiosity and the mysteries of the cosmos. Continuing the program, the Deputy President of XAS, Dr. Suparna Roychowdhury, delivered her inspiring address, bridging the theme of the event and the motivation behind it. It was followed by the address of the Assistant Director of FELO and Head of the Department of Physics, Dr. Shibaji Banerjee, compelling the audience to a verbal tour of the history of FELO and Fr. Eugene Lafont's contributions - emphasizing the importance of astronomy as a way of nurturing wonder and humility.







The felicitation of the day’s esteemed speaker, Dr. Sumanta Chakraborty, Assistant Professor, Indian Association for the Cultivation of Science (IACS), Kolkata, was carried out with warmth and gratitude. The student core committee of the society was officially announced, highlighting a moment of pride for all who had worked tirelessly behind the scenes. Dr. Chakraborty then took the stage for his speaker session, sharing his insights on “Have We Understood Gravity?”. He explored the journey of gravitational physics, from the pioneers who built its foundations to modern advances like the Event Horizon Telescope and gravitational wave detectors. He highlighted unanswered questions that still shape the field, reminding us that gravity, though the oldest known force, continues to present new mysteries. His delightful talk with the audience inspired them with his deep knowledge and passion for astrophysics. Dr. Chakraborty’s session was followed by a question-and-answer session concerning the contents of the same, which was conducted by Dr. Sarbari Guha, Associate Professor in the Postgraduate and Research Department of Physics, St. Xavier’s College (Autonomous), Kolkata.







One of the most unique parts of the day was the mock courtroom session titled “The Cosmic Colloquy: Section 299,792C.” This was a vivid dramatization that brought philosophical giants into dialogue about the universe and its meaning towards humankind. On one side stood the Petitioner: Council for Scientific Rationality (CSR), who called upon Dr. Bertrand Russell as their witness, representing a rational, critical perspective rooted in logic. On the other side, the Respondent: Coalition for Human Spirit and Culture (CHSC) was represented by none other than Kaviguru Rabindranath Tagore, whose poetic wisdom provided a soulful counterbalance.







The clash of ideas in this imaginative courtroom allowed the participants to reflect on science not just as equations and data but as a dialogue with human thought, creativity, and belief. The day concluded with the Vote of Thanks delivered by the Assistant Secretary, who summed up the proceedings and expressed gratitude to everyone present. The second day of the program, held on the 20th of September at Dr. Ranjan Ray Lab for Computational Physics, was dedicated more directly to astrophysical science. The session began with the Former Graduate Research Student in the Indian Institute of Science (IISc) and Indian Institute of Astrophysics (IIA), Bangalore, Dr. Samriddhi Sankar Maity’s wonderful discussion on solar cycles and solar magnetohydrodynamics. His lecture illuminated the delicate balance maintained by the Sun’s magnetic field, the phenomenon of solar flares, and their profound effects on Earth. He explained how these solar events sustain our atmosphere and even life itself, while also addressing the dangers they pose through magnetic storms and bursts of energy. The anatomy of solar flares, the dynamics of solar bursts, and the complex interplay of plasma and magnetism were discussed with clarity. For the students, it was a reminder of how our existence is intimately tied to processes unfolding millions of kilometers away in the heart of our star.




After a short break, the focus shifted to stellar astrophysics under the guidance of Dr. Gourab Banerjee, Post Doctoral Fellow at the Indian Institute of Astrophysics (IIA), Bangalore, and Vainu Bappu Observatory, Kavalpur. His session, divided into two halves before and after lunch, was both rich in history and full of cutting-edge insights. He traced the journey of stellar astrophysics from its origins, explaining how stars were once thought to be composed of iron before the revolutionary discovery that hydrogen was their primary element. The classification of stars by their spectra was explained in depth, with real examples such as Sirius, Canopus, and Regulus. By showing their spectral plots, Dr. Banerjee revealed how elemental absorption lines carry the secrets of stellar composition. The discussion on classical Be stars, with their unique emission spectra and the presence of a decretion disc of orbiting gas, unlike ordinary stars, displayed dynamic and complex features that make them fascinating to study. Dr. Banerjee also shared methods of detecting and analyzing these stellar phenomena, from spectral extraction techniques to the role of CCDs in capturing raw data, which must then be refined into usable scientific information. His account of his ongoing research at IIA and the Vainu Bappu Observatory provided a glimpse into the frontier of modern astrophysical study.







Together, these two days exemplified the spirit of the Xaverian Astronomical Society, framing a blend of scientific rigor, creative imagination, and deep human curiosity to reach for uncertainties, even where “light travels, voices don’t”. Whether through the debate of the cosmic mock courtroom or the technical details of gravity, solar magnetohydrodynamics, and stellar spectra, the sessions were united by one theme — the desire to understand the universe in all of its dimensions; to decode the Sidera Silentia: The Silence between the Stars.
